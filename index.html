Rethinking the narrative

AI is a tool for healing the planet

Artificial intelligence gets a bad reputation for its energy use, but the full picture tells a very different story. From protecting rainforests to predicting climate disasters, AI is becoming one of our most powerful environmental allies.

Common misconceptions

Myth

"AI is too energy-hungry to be green"

Training large AI models does use energy, but once trained, models run efficiently. AI-driven grid optimization already saves far more energy than it consumes.

Myth

"AI will replace environmentalists"

AI is a tool, not a replacement. It amplifies what scientists, activists, and conservationists can do, processing satellite data in hours that would take years manually.

Myth

"Tech companies don't care about carbon"

Many AI labs now run on renewable energy. Google, Microsoft, and others have made binding net-zero commitments and are using AI to meet them faster.

Fact

AI actively reduces emissions in power grids

Google's DeepMind reduced data center cooling energy by 40% using AI. Smart grid AI cuts waste at a national scale, 24 hours a day.

Fact

AI detects illegal deforestation in real time

Rainforest monitoring systems like Global Forest Watch use machine learning to flag illegal logging within hours of it happening, anywhere on Earth.

Fact

AI models can predict extreme weather weeks ahead

New AI-based weather models outperform traditional physics-based systems, giving communities more time to prepare for floods, hurricanes, and droughts.

Oceans

AI maps coral reef health, tracks illegal fishing, and detects plastic pollution across millions of miles of ocean.

Marine conservation

How AI is involved

AI systems process enormous volumes of sonar data, underwater imagery, and satellite feeds to monitor ocean health at a scale no human team could manage alone. Deep learning models identify coral bleaching events early, allowing scientists to respond before damage becomes irreversible. Computer vision trained on drone footage classifies ocean plastic by type and density, informing cleanup operations. AI-powered vessel monitoring systems cross-reference GPS signals with fishing licenses in real time to flag illegal trawling anywhere in the world.

Real-world project

Global Fishing Watch

This open-access platform uses AI to analyze AIS vessel tracking data from over 65,000 ships worldwide. Its machine learning models detect suspicious patterns indicating illegal fishing and have helped governments prosecute violations in international waters, contributing to protected marine areas in Chile, Ecuador, and across West Africa.

🌳

Forests

Machine learning monitors tree cover loss in near real-time and acoustic AI listens for illegal chainsaw activity in rainforests.

Deforestation detection

How AI is involved

Forests cover roughly 31% of Earth's land surface, making manual monitoring impossible. AI systems analyze multispectral satellite imagery from sources like Landsat and Sentinel-2 to detect tree cover changes within days of them occurring. Beyond visual data, acoustic monitoring AI classifies sounds in the forest, distinguishing bird calls from chainsaws, trucks, and gunshots with high accuracy. Natural language processing tools also scan trade and customs databases to flag timber shipments that may have originated from illegally logged forests.

Real-world project

Rainforest Connection (RFCx)

RFCx deploys repurposed smartphones in forest canopies that stream audio to cloud-based AI models. These models detect illegal logging sounds in real time and alert rangers via SMS within minutes. The system is active in over 20 countries and has helped indigenous communities protect their land without requiring them to be present in the forest at all times.

🦁

Wildlife

Computer vision identifies individual animals from camera traps and AI helps rangers predict poaching before it happens.

Species monitoring

How AI is involved

Wildlife conservationists often rely on camera traps, which can generate hundreds of thousands of images per week. AI models classify species, count individuals, and identify specific animals by unique markings such as stripe patterns on tigers or spot configurations on leopards, turning months of manual tagging into hours of automated analysis. Predictive AI systems go further by analyzing poaching incident records, patrol routes, terrain data, and weather to forecast where poachers are most likely to strike, allowing rangers to be deployed proactively rather than reactively.

Real-world project

PAWS (Protection Assistant for Wildlife Security)

Developed by USC and now used by the WWF and various national parks, PAWS uses a game-theoretic AI model to predict poacher behavior and optimize ranger patrol schedules. Field tests in Uganda and Cambodia showed a measurable reduction in poaching activity in areas where PAWS-guided patrols were deployed.

🌡️

Climate predictions

AI climate models simulate ocean-atmosphere interactions and project long-term warming with unprecedented speed and accuracy.

Climate science

How AI is involved

Traditional climate models use physics equations to simulate the atmosphere and are computationally expensive, sometimes taking weeks to run on supercomputers. AI-based models, trained on decades of historical climate data, can run equivalent simulations thousands of times faster. This allows scientists to test far more scenarios and produce more granular regional forecasts. Neural networks are also being used to improve flood mapping, predict wildfire spread, and model sea level rise at the local level, turning broad global projections into actionable local intelligence.

Real-world project

Google DeepMind's GraphCast

GraphCast is an AI weather prediction model that outperformed the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts model in over 90% of tested variables. It produces 10-day global weather forecasts in under a minute on a single computer, compared to hours of supercomputer time for traditional models. Its improved accuracy gives disaster response teams earlier warning of extreme events like hurricanes and heat waves.

Disease

AI tracks climate-linked disease outbreaks and monitors ecosystem stress that signals emerging infectious threats.

Epidemiology

How AI is involved

As temperatures rise, mosquito habitats expand into new regions, bringing diseases like dengue, malaria, and Zika with them. AI systems correlate climate data, land use changes, and historical outbreak records to predict where these diseases will appear next, allowing health ministries to deploy preventive measures before outbreaks begin. Beyond vector-borne illness, AI monitors the health of wildlife populations and ecosystems. When animal diversity drops or species flee an area, it often precedes the spillover of animal viruses into human populations, and AI makes these warning signals detectable at a global scale.

Real-world project

HealthMap (Boston Children's Hospital)

HealthMap is an AI-powered disease surveillance platform that aggregates data from news reports, social media, official health alerts, and eyewitness accounts in dozens of languages. It was one of the first systems to detect the 2014 Ebola outbreak in West Africa and flagged early signals of COVID-19 in Wuhan before official announcements were made, monitoring over 200 diseases across 200 countries in real time.

🌾

Agriculture

Precision farming AI cuts fertilizer use by up to 25%, optimizes irrigation, and monitors crop health with drone imagery.

Sustainable farming

How AI is involved

Agriculture accounts for roughly 70% of global freshwater use and is a major source of greenhouse gas emissions through overuse of fertilizers and inefficient machinery. AI addresses this from multiple angles. Computer vision models mounted on drones or tractors identify pest infestations and nutrient deficiencies at the plant level, allowing targeted treatment rather than blanket application of chemicals. Soil sensors combined with machine learning optimize irrigation schedules by predicting exactly when and where water is needed. AI also forecasts crop yields months in advance, reducing food waste by helping supply chains plan more accurately.

Real-world project

Plantix by PEAT

Plantix is a smartphone app used by over 10 million farmers that uses AI image recognition to diagnose crop diseases, pest infestations, and nutrient deficiencies from a single photo. Operating in over 18 languages across India, Indonesia, and sub-Saharan Africa, it gives smallholder farmers instant expert-level guidance, reducing unnecessary pesticide use and improving yields without additional chemical inputs.
